Opening a business: own it yourself, legally

You can own 100% of a Saudi company in most sectors, sponsor your own iqama through it, and bring your family. That is the real route. It costs more than the shortcuts people whisper about in the groups, and unlike them it does not end with a prison sentence.

The MISA route, step by step

The Ministry of Investment (MISA) registers foreign investors. Since the 2025 investment law it is a simplified registration rather than the old licensing regime, and the government fees for the standard license are currently waived, though that can change without notice. You will typically need a company in your home country with a year of financials behind it.

The sequence: MISA registration, then commercial registration with the Ministry of Commerce through the Saudi Business Center, then chamber of commerce, national address, tax registration, the Qiwa and GOSI accounts, a municipal license, and finally your own iqama as general manager of your company. Six to twelve weeks is realistic with everything in order. Budget SAR 15,000–25,000 for the first year in registrations, address and processing costs even with the license fee waived, plus whatever your actual business needs.

For startup founders there is a separate entrepreneur license, endorsed by an approved incubator or VC, and it opens the door to the entrepreneur track of Premium Residency. Funded founders should look hard at that combination.

What is open, what is not

Most sectors allow full foreign ownership: trading, services, IT, manufacturing, education, healthcare, logistics, construction. A restricted list remains, and one entry matters for this audience: Hajj and Umrah services in the holy cities are reserved. If your business dream is a pilgrim-services company in Makkah, you need Saudi partners through a genuine, registered joint venture, and you should talk to us before spending anything.

Official starting points: misa.gov.sa, business.sa and, for small-business support programs, monshaat.gov.sa. A business visit visa (about SAR 535 as an eVisa) is the right tool for a scouting trip to meet lawyers and potential partners; it does not permit work or convert to residency.

The shortcut that is a crime

Tasattur. "Find a Saudi, he opens the business in his name in five minutes, you run it and pay him monthly." You will hear this advice in every hijrah group, sometimes from people who mean well. It is called commercial concealment, tasattur, and it is a crime for both parties under the Anti-Concealment Law: up to five years in prison, fines up to SAR 5 million, confiscation of everything the business made, and for you deportation with a permanent ban. Enforcement is real and has been aggressive since 2021.

The bitter irony is that the legal route now costs less than most people pay their "partner" in a single year. Fear Allah in how you earn, and build something your children can inherit.
