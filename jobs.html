Working in Saudi Arabia: what is actually open in 2026

A job offer is still the main road to hijrah: the employer handles your visa and iqama, salaries are tax-free, and skilled roles let you bring your family. But Saudization has changed the market, and you should know what is open and what is closed before you spend months applying.

Open doors and closed doors

The government is reserving more and more roles for Saudi citizens. As of 2026 that includes most HR, accounting, sales, customer service, reception and general admin positions. If your CV says "office manager", the Saudi market is not looking for you.

What stays open, and pays well, is skilled work the country imports: medicine and nursing, engineering of every kind, senior software and cybersecurity, university teaching, aviation, hospitality management, and English teaching. The giga-projects around the Hijaz, including the developments in Makkah and Madinah, hire internationally. Western passport holders are usually processed in the "high-skilled" permit tier, which expects a degree and a salary of SAR 15,000 or more.

A licensed teacher couple can realistically save 100,000 riyals a year while living ten minutes from the Haram in Jeddah. That is the honest appeal of this route.

Where to actually apply

LinkedIn and Bayt carry most professional hiring. GulfTalent is good for mid-to-senior roles and vets its employers. Naukrigulf is strong in engineering, IT and healthcare, and Indeed aggregates the rest. Expatriates.com has an active Jeddah section but it is unmoderated classifieds, so assume scams exist there. For teaching, apply directly to international schools (our schools page lists the main ones) and to universities.

Old lists in the community still pass around sites that are dead or parked. Save your time: apply through the boards above, or ask in the jobs group where members post openings.

Before you sign anything

Three checks that save families. First, the employer pays visa and iqama fees by law (Labor Law, Article 40); if they ask you to pay, walk away. Second, confirm your contract is registered in the government's Qiwa system once you start; an unregistered contract means no legal protection. Third, confirm your job title is on the list that allows family sponsorship before you sign. A "technician" title can trap a degree-holder into years without his wife and kids.
No "free visa". Paying a Saudi to sponsor you while you freelance around is illegal, with fines up to SAR 100,000 and deportation, details on the visas page. And working remotely for a foreign company while living here on visit visas is a grey zone with no legal cover; the clean version of that life is Premium Residency.
